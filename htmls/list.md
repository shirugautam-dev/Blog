# I Was on the List

## A second chance I did not expect

![](./Images/articles/list.jpg)

### 
When the news was full of ICE crackdowns in the US, one memory kept coming back to me — my own ordeal with Australian immigration in 1999, a year after I landed in Sydney.

It had been very hot that November, and I had just finished my shift at the hotel where I used to be a casual worker, filling in for whoever had taken leave. I was hardly going to the business school I was admitted to, and when I did, it would be to mark myself present for all the days. Most of my friends, who were mainly from South Asia, were doing the same. The school did not object to that either.

A friend and co-worker was waiting for me outside the hotel. We used to catch a train to college after the shift, his school nearby mine.

He looked distressed, and as soon as I came out, he blurted, “They caught Manoj* as well.” Manoj* was another friend from Nepal. We came to Sydney with the same dream — study, find good jobs, build a future. But the schools we were admitted to were not recognised, and we felt cheated and demoralised.

During that time, immigration was going door to door, targeting students who were not attending classes or working beyond allowed hours. Many like us were caught in it. Some were hiding and looking for lawyers to represent them. Some were deported, and some were in detention centres.

Manoj* was smart. I knew he would find a way out. And he did — I heard later he found a lawyer and managed to stay. Not everyone was so lucky.

“Don’t worry, he will figure out”, I replied, not knowing that the same fate was waiting for me as well.

I was enrolled in a Diploma in Computer Science at Hurstville Business School, which was to close within a year and a half, the principal to become a fugitive, not knowing that it would be a waste of my time and my parents’ hard-earned money. I had already paid a year’s fees and planned to move to a recognised university. Until then, I worked full-time and attended classes just enough to stay out of trouble.

Once we reached Hurstville, my friend and I promised to meet again at the train station after a couple of hours. On that day, the school was uncannily silent. The classes were empty; there was just one girl in the reception. As soon as she saw me, she said, “Your name is also in the list.”

“What list?” I could not make out what she was talking about.

“List from the immigration. They enquired about you”

The ground shifted beneath me. For a moment, I could not hear anything. The room did not feel the same anymore. My hands were wet, and I kept wiping them on my jeans. My face burned, my heart pounding.

“What do I do now? Who do I even speak to? Should I leave? Should I stay? What if they come looking for me?” Thoughts were racing inside me.

“You can call your case officer”. In a yellow sticky note, she gave me a number.

With my head spinning and a shaky hand, I dialled the number. I don’t remember the exact words that came out of my mouth, but I remember my case officer telling me to meet her the next day in her office.

“Just go and speak to your case officer tomorrow. Do not let them come looking for you.” My mother said when I called her that night, crying. And I did exactly that.

Luckily for me, just a week back, I had finished all the formalities of the admission in University of Western Sydney to study a Bachelor’s in IT, starting in February 2000.

The next day, I took the admission letter, the fee receipt and left the house. When I stepped out, the same road felt different. Nothing had changed, and yet everything had. I thought about the people who were taken and deported. I prayed, probably even cut a deal with God. When I reached the immigration office, I waited more than 45 minutes before being called inside.

My case officer was a kind-looking middle-aged lady. She observed me for a few seconds before saying anything, as if she could see how nervous I was. She offered me water, and we started talking.

I laid out everything on the table, the documents from Uni, trying to prove that I was serious about studying. I told her how I was duped by the school into believing that it was recognised, and my only motive was to study. She asked me about my plans, and I told her I would return once I complete my degree.

At the end, she said, “I am happy you came here today. And this is your last warning. I hope not to see your name again. Study well and best of luck.”

I came out and sat on a bench for a long time. People walked past as if it were just another day. For me, it did not feel like one. I was still trembling. I could not believe what had happened. I was given a second chance.

And I was to make the best of it.

Sometimes I try to think of an alternate life where I was deported. What kind of life would I have lived? Certainly, not the one that I have built now. The country I now call home, the person I have become, would be different. A few minutes in that office decided the life I got to live.

That day, I learnt something I did not understand then. Running away only delays fear and probably amplifies it even. Facing it, even trembling, changes something inside you.
