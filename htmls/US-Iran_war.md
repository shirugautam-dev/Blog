# US, Israel-Iran War Is Psychological Destruction

### 
I am an ordinary person living a cosy life in India. Life has been the same for me for a long time. I spend my day with the same ritual of cooking, eating, reading, writing, exercising and then scrolling. I try to limit the scrolling part, telling myself not to get caught in the rabbit hole. These days, though, I find myself spending a bit more time on the news app than others.

Indian news is mostly about politicians — who said what, not who did what, cricket matches or the players, and entertainment. I am not interested in any of these. I do not want to know which politician criticised which politician in another party for something silly, that could be ignored, but they want it to go viral. I have understood the politics of optics — if they have to look nice, they have to make others look bad. Cricket matches are always happening, some IPL here or some test match there. One cannot be interested in it all the time. I am also not curious about the lifestyle of the stars. Let them be. I have my own life to live. The thing that I am interested in is what is happening in the world that affects all the ordinaries like me.

US, Isreal — Iran war has caught everyone’s attention. The war itself is being stalled; no one knows for how long. One cannot believe what Donald Trump is saying on his X account or Truth Social. One day, he says he won, another day he says he will attack Iran in one hour. I look at my watch and think, “Should I put a timer?” Then there are tweets from Iranian Embassies. Mostly, they are hilarious, and I laugh. But it is not a laugh of happiness. It has a sorrow underneath. When they talk about missiles and threats, like they are playing some video games, they make me nervous. The uncertainty of what will happen tomorrow, with what news I will wake up, makes me uncomfortable. The whole day I move around as if nothing has happened, but my mind is not at ease. I think about the worst case, the best case and the status quo. I am not able to convince myself that it is not going to affect me.

Then I check the apps where I have invested in SIPs and a few stocks. They seem fine for now. I need not worry, but the confusion remains. Though the war feels distant, it still enters my mind and takes hold. I can feel the anxiety in me, suppressed by my own false conviction of normalcy. “All is well”, I say.

I have too much information, yet I am clueless. I cannot make out what is true, what is exaggerated and what is theatrical. Every advisor and commentator who comes on YouTube podcasts has conflicting advice and opinions. I wish they would all sit in one place and gain the insight, decide what is true, and then come out in public and give one statement. I know that is silly of me. Why would they do that? Of course, it has to be different. They may be as clueless as me or they have to make headlines.

For me, inflation, jobs, petrol prices, the stock market and the economy matter. I want to know if my food prices will go up, do I need to give more money to my son for petrol, or shall I tell him to stay home, will my son get a job once he graduates next month, and is my money in the stocks safe? Will RBI intervene to strengthen the Indian rupee, or will it let the market forces decide?

I want someone to give me the answers.

Some countries are in war, but all others are also feeling the effects of it. The fear of economic collapse, cyber-attacks, nuclear attacks and eventually a world war looms.

I constantly refresh news feeds. I see graphic footage of explosions, repeated endlessly, and that remains with me for days. I realise that the fear is sold in the name of news; even so, I cannot let go of the anxiety. I live thousands of kilometres away from the war, but it still becomes part of me. I cannot escape.

The fear that is created by this war is perhaps the biggest tragedy because fearful minds live in confusion, in conflict and therefore must be violent, distorted and aggressive, as Jiddu Krishnamurthy rightly said, and they can only destroy further.
