# A Life, Then Silence

## What Death Reveals About Who We Are, What We Own, and the Stories We Tell Ourselves

### subheading 
The one call we had been dreading finally came. My husband picked up the call. His sister was crying on the other end — brother-in-law had passed away. He was battling fourth-stage lung cancer. He fought hard for over a year, went through several rounds of chemo and immunotherapy. But at the end, he left us.
The man who lived to eat, who loved to travel and who laughed the loudest was no more. “I think 2026 will be a better year for me. The whole of 2025, I was not well”, were his words to me when I visited him just two months earlier.
His death brought a realisation that I cannot shake off: what we call ours is not ours to have forever.

### Life Goes On
It does not matter whether we are prepared for death or it is sudden; death itself is final. There is nothing after death. Those who remain keep on living. The world does not stop. It is indifferent. It keeps spinning.
We do not pause because someone died. Very little seems to change. We go back to our routine life. The traffic keeps on moving. The office fills up. Someone cracks a joke at lunch. After a few days or weeks, everything becomes normal again. That normalcy itself feels shocking.

### What Remains?
Now I ask: who died other than the body? The name that was given to him when he was born? The roles he took on? The achievements he gathered? They all dissolved as soon as he died. There is no name, no father, no husband or any other relation. The certificates he collected have no meaning now. The money he saved, others will enjoy. The house he built, others will live in. He did not take anything with him. He came empty-handed and left the same way.
We take life so seriously that we forget how abruptly it can end.
For those of us who are left behind, he now exists only as a collection of memories. And those memories are fragile and fade faster than we expect. His laugh is already harder to recall than two weeks ago.
Then what remains? The impact that he made? The love he gave? Or is there nothing that truly persists? I do not have the answers, but the questions remain.

### Rituals, Belief and Fear
What started with an immense sense of loss and confusion ended with a myriad of rituals. Rituals did provide some structure during chaos. Everyone got busy with what to do next. Call relatives, arrange for the funeral. But all during that, there was unquestioned fear and superstition. What is to be done so that the soul rests, or what is not to be done so that nothing bad will happen to the living souls?
I wonder now who the ritual was for. For the one who died or the living ones, managing their fear? Were we trying to get rid of the anxiety about death by doing all those rituals? Do we believe in heaven and hell? Do these rituals guide the deceased somewhere, or do they comfort those left behind? Questions about the soul, its existence, and its place began to surface.
I wonder if we were honouring the person or addressing our own fears.
 
Beneath all of it, I sensed not just belief — but a deep fear of death itself.

### Fear vs Reality
We all fear death. We try not to talk about it. We rarely say “dead.” We soften it — “passed away,” “no more” — as if language can reduce its finality. Yet it happens to every one of us.
We simply leave one day. And when that day comes, we do not know anything.
Death is an absence, not an experience.
One who dies does not know that he died. So, the fear seems to be larger than the reality. The very thing that we fear the most is something we will never experience. The horror of death is an imagined one. The death itself is quiet stillness.

### Futility of Our Plans
Throughout our lives, we plan, secure, gather and worry. We act as if life is stable. We worry about trivial things; hold on to plans as if they are guarantees; want what we don’t have, only to leave behind everything.
He had plans, too. A trip he was planning to take once he got better, a dish he wanted to eat again. 2026 was going to be better.
None of it happened, though. The future plans of buying a new house, a new car mean nothing now. The carefully built routines that were supposed to give a long, healthy life dissolved overnight.
Death exposes how we own nothing at the end.

### An Awareness That Fades
I was expecting a major change in myself after seeing the death from such a close distance. I expected to become less anxious. To be more aware of the time, to be more present.
I expected a transformation, but instead, I found only an awareness that comes and goes.
Life resumes as it always does. Only now, I notice how fragile that continuity is.
And maybe that’s enough. Not a transformation — just a small, recurring reminder: that I, too, will end. That the person next to me will not always be there.
The man who thought 2026 would be a better year could not see it. And one day, without knowing it, neither will I.
The awareness lingers and fades. But while it’s there, it’s worth listening to.
