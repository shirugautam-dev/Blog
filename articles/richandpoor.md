# I Am Not Rich, She Is Not Poor

![](../Images/articles/richandpoor.jpg)
###
Anyone who watched Deuba couple’s amassed riches burning during the Gen Z protest in Nepal in September 2025 must have been astonished by the view. The cash burning, gold and silver melting and their house ablaze along with them. It was not a sight one gets to see every day, but that day was not every day, and those are not ordinary couples. They are the ex-PM and the ex-FM of Nepal.

I rewatched the videos many times, every time, shocked. What were they thinking? After all, who needs that kind of money, for what reasons and by what means? How much money does one need before one feels it is enough?

Yesterday, I asked my domestic help, “Sagar, if you win a lottery of 1 crore, what would you do?”

She smiled.
“How many zeros, didi?”

I grabbed a piece of paper, and I wrote one. Then I started adding zeros one by one, every time looking at her. Till it was four, she was smiling, but after that, she was in shock. By the time I reached seventh, she was giggling.

“That much didi!”, she yelled.
“I will just go to my village and never come back. I will never work.”
“No house in the city?” I asked.
“No.”
“Is that it? Nothing else?”
“No.”

She did not hesitate. There was no confusion. She knew exactly what was missing in her life. Comfort of not working as a helper at others’ homes. And she knew that the money could give her that.

Then it was my turn to answer the same question to myself. Unlike her, mine was a lukewarm response. There was no excitement in me. Of course, I would be happy, but all I could think of was that I would invest it.

In that moment, I got uncomfortable with myself, and hence started justifying it. I told myself that it was for future security, and immediately, I could see the fallacy of that argument. I already have a house to live in, a car to drive, plenty of food at my table and more than enough to meet my other needs. Yet, I was planning for more. I was not dreaming about living differently; I was dreaming about gathering more.

Sagar does not need more. She needs money to meet her basic needs. If that money is available, she will choose to live her life in comfort. But here I am, still lacking despite living comfortably. My relationship with money is different from hers. Instead of enjoying it, I want to grow it. More security, more return, more wealth. How much will be enough? Will it ever be enough?

So, between the two of us, who is poorer?

Sagar is poor in worldly sense, but she knows how much is enough. I am materially comfortable, yet I cannot define my enough.

Perhaps most of us live our lives like that. For years, we earn, save and invest believing that one more flat, one more promotion, a better car will make our future safe. We make earning our primary objective. We forget that wealth is a means to an end, not an end itself.

How did we reach here? Maybe the answer lies in the perpetual insecurity we humans live with. Money does give us security to some extent, and it becomes tempting to chase it more and more.

Saving is important. It protects us during difficult times and gives us peace of mind. But when saving turns into hoarding, we start compromising on health, relationships and the present moment. When we start living to earn, not earning to live, we need to question what we are saving for.

Sagar knows where the journey can end, while I am still changing my goalpost. What I already have is not helping me feel wealthy.

Maybe poverty is not only about not having enough money but also about the inability to feel that we already have enough.

Yesterday I realised that I am not rich and she is not poor.
