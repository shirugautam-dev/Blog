# The Strange Comfort of Keeping Busy

## On leaving a job, filling the silence, and what I found when I stopped.

### 
I’ve been free for a few months now. Not that I spend my days sleeping or binge-watching Netflix. I quit my job and stepped into what felt, at first, like unstructured time. What I did not expect was how quickly I would fill it, and what that would teach me about myself.
I filled my days with reading, writing and unexpectedly, building my own website from scratch.
My days are packed. Cooking and going to the gym take a good chunk of my time. Then I sit with my laptop, tweaking bits of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. I love how a small change in code can transform an entire page — something a friend called inevitable. ‘Once a coder, always a coder,’ she said. I’m starting to think she was right.
I write in between — just this and that. And I read, fiction, non-fiction, whatever my mood demands.
I thought I would feel a vacuum after quitting. People said I would. But I haven’t felt it yet. My days pass quite nicely. I expected boredom. Instead, I am having more fun.
No time pressure. Just a commitment to myself. And somehow, I keep my hands full.
There is a strange comfort in being busy.

### Why Being Busy Feels So Good?

Being busy makes me feel like I am doing something right. Before getting out of bed, I make a mental note of chores I need to finish before hitting the gym. I need not do that anymore. I can get up, have a cup of coffee in silence. I can slowly immerse myself in the day without being anxious about the pending tasks. But that does not happen. Naturally, I get into the warrior mode.
And I don’t think it’s just me.
Work makes us look responsible. We sound ambitious. It feels like we are making progress, even when we are not entirely sure towards what. Busyness gives our days a shape. It gives us a sense of identity.
It is also convenient.
If we are always doing something, we don’t have to listen to our thoughts. We don’t have to sit with doubts or uncertainty. We don’t have to ask ourselves if we are actually satisfied.
 
We start believing that movement is meaning.
And somewhere along the way, being busy becomes a badge of honour. No one asked us to wear it, but we do it anyway. We carry exhaustion like proof — proof that we matter, that we are needed.
But needed by whom?

### When Busyness Becomes Avoidance

The comfort of being busy comes with a cost.
It lets us escape.
It distracts from difficult decisions; from things we don’t quite want to face. During the day, we stay occupied. But at night, when everything is quiet, those thoughts return. They were only waiting for us to listen to them. An old decision that we still think was not good, a small comment someone made during the day, an event that we still have not made peace with.
Maybe that’s why we avoid stillness. Because when we are still, we see things clearly. And clarity can be uncomfortable.
We tell ourselves we are being productive. But sometimes, we are just postponing something important. Something harder.
Why do we feel like we need to earn rest through exhaustion? Think about it. We never say, “I rested well today” with the same pride as “I got so much done today”. Rest without effort feels like cheating, like we have not earned it. Maybe we were never really taught that resting is legitimate on its own. Value comes from output, and the one who produces more is respected more. Is it because we are conditioned to believe that we need to work to feel our worth?
But the truth is, the busier we become, the less present we are. We stretch ourselves across too many things and end up being fully present in none. Productivity becomes easier than presence.
And yet, even after doing so much, there is often a strange emptiness.
 

### What Stillness Does

So how much is enough? And who decides that? Not just in terms of time and effort, but also money, possessions, and expectations. We rarely stop to ask.

So, I tried it. I closed the laptop. I didn’t read, didn’t cook, didn’t put on music. Just sat for a day with nothing on my to-do list. I just lay on the bed, sat on the sofa, walked around the park and watched out from my window. I took long showers and took even longer to dress up. It was uncomfortable at first. I am not used to this much stillness. My mind kept reaching for something to do. But I let it settle. I held the urge to jump into something and just do it.
And then things became clearer. Not dramatic revelations. Just small, honest things. That I was enjoying this period of my life more than I admitted out loud. That some of my busyness was genuine, and some of it was just habit. That I didn’t need to justify my days to anyone — including myself.

That was enough.
