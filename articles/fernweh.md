# Why We Feel Life Is Better Somewhere Else


![](../Images/articles/fernweh.jpg)
###
A couple of times a month, my son says he can’t wait to get out of this city. He complains about his college, the teachers, the traffic, and the weather. He thinks his friends from Bangalore and Hyderabad are enjoying life more than him. His Japanese friend seems lucky to him. His cousin in Germany has a perfect life. He talks about places he has never been but only seen on screen. He listens to the news that is happening in faraway places and wishes that it would happen here as well.

He asks, “Why did you guys not have me in Sydney? Why did you move back?”

He is sometimes impressed by New York and sometimes by Munich. He cannot decide where he wants to be. It changes every few months. My heart clenches every time I hear that. I wish he would stop saying that, even better, not go anywhere. Stay with me forever. In response, I give a nonchalant shrug. Only I know how much it takes to let go of him. It takes everything.

But I understand him. I can relate to the ache. I had felt the same 30 years back. I wanted to get away from Kathmandu. Get away to a far land where I imagined life would be better. Just by escaping Kathmandu, I would be happy. It could be any place, the US, Australia, the UK, Canada, anywhere but Kathmandu.

I told everyone I wanted to study well, and that could happen only somewhere else. I told them there was no future in Nepal. I told them that the system sucked, just like my son now says. But now I know, more than anything, how frivolous that was. I was missing a place I had not even been before. All reasons were mere excuses.
 
Germans have a word for it, Fernweh. It is the opposite of homesickness. It is farsickness. Fren means far away, and weh means ache, longing. It is that yearning for a place you have never visited but only imagined. The feeling is mostly observed in people going through a major life crisis, or when one is not happy with their job or in early adulthood or midlife crisis. A life that is different from the one living looks much more attractive, and the longing becomes painful.

Every now and then we feel like going on vacation, get out of our routine life. But Fernweh is much more than that; it is like a calling. One can feel that the place is where he belongs once he starts looking at maps, checking photos and reading travel blogs. Nostalgia hits like a storm, and the melancholic mind thinks the only fix is to get there. A better life is waiting somewhere else.

In all this, one is not looking for a place to go but to escape one’s reality. It is a quest for freedom. Freedom from the past. A need to run away and reinvent oneself by escaping the present.

But there is no escape. Once you move to that place, that land also becomes boring and dull after some time. Nothing can keep you excited forever. Once routine sets up, life gets monotonous, and the ache returns. Again, it starts looking for a faraway land to move to, a different life to live. Fernweh sets in again.

I want to tell my son that this is all temporary. I have gone through the same thing he is going through now. There is no perfect place where you will always be satisfied. There is no perfect system. There is no perfect life. Anywhere you go, you will still feel the same. After some time, every new becomes old. Job starts feeling shitty. City becomes chaotic. Weather changes. Preferences change. The yearning to move comes back again. How much can you run? How far will you go?

The real freedom is in your mind, not in the cities or the weather. Anywhere you go, you carry your mind with you. So, look into it and come out of the yearning.

But I don’t say anything because I know he has to go through it himself. He has to go and live a life elsewhere to know that the place does not matter, weather does not matter. He has to learn this himself, like I did.
