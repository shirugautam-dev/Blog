# The Versions of Myself I have Missed

![](../Images/articles/versions.jpg)

###
One evening in the summer of 2016, my husband and I were having dinner with some friends. On that night, we forgot we were adults. We talked, laughed and pulled each other’s leg.We were children in adult bodies. Suddenly, one of us remembered the “Jimmy, Jimmy, Jimmy, aajaa, aajaa, aajaa” song from Disco Dancer, and one of my friends and I just got up and danced like it was our only chance, singing loudly, not bothering about the tune or the voice. We thought that phase of life would last forever.

When I think about that night, I wonder what got to me. I have always had apprehensions when it came to dancing. Singing, I don’t mind, but people around me do. But that night I was not myself. There was no fear of being judged or embarrassment of any kind. At that time, I assumed it was the company, only to realise later it was not just that. I have had the same company many times before and many times after, but that very scene was never repeated. It was one of a kind.

I moved away from that house a couple of years later and did not meet them for quite a long time.

Just a month back, I met five other ladies for dinner in a restaurant. Among them, three were from that night in 2016.It was bound to be fun.We talked, we laughed, and we teased each other. We were loud enough to get annoyed glances from other diners. We reminisced and talked about where life has taken each of us. We shared pictures of our grown-up children and lamented how fast they grew. We chuckled that our spouses looked older than we remembered and secretly took a little pleasure in it. It was one of those nights when you enjoy without trying.

While coming back, I started thinking how this time was different from the one years ago. It was the same group of people. We laughed just as much; we teased just as mercilessly. We forgot about anything else for a couple of hours. We had fun, but still something was missing.
 
My friend could not just get up and dance, and I could not sing in my raspy voice. We had all grown a bit older. Our phones were within reach, expecting calls from home anytime. One started thinking about the ride home a little too early as she stayed far; another was a bit quieter than before. We gave our all but still held some.

I thought it was age. The responsibilities. Maybe we have grown to be more sensible. Perhaps this is how it is. Growing older made us more deliberate, less impulsive. A few days later, it struck me. I was not missing my friends. I was missing the version of myself when I was with them at that time. Same me, same them, but a different time could not give the vibe of that night of 2016. With the years passing by, we had changed; no one was the same anymore.

Every relationship brings out a different version of us. In me resides a mother, a daughter, a wife, a friend, a neighbour, a customer, and even a stranger. None is a pretence; I am genuinely all of them. We may feel like we have a certain personality, but the people around us and the phase of life we are in shape us constantly. With my parents, I am just a daughter. With my son, I am a mother first. With friends, I am still the same crazy woman who sometimes blurts out things that make their faces blush. While shopping, I am a difficult customer one day, and the next day I am sympathetic towards those young assistants. I am not always kind, nor always impatient. I can be very understanding in one situation; in another, I can be unforgiving. In some versions, I am carefree; in some, I am cautious. Some versions laugh louder; Some versions lie awake at night with anxiety. Different companies, different places, different times bring out different versions of me and each of them is real.

My childhood friends, my grandparents, my first job, and my old neighbourhood all often make me nostalgic. I say I miss them. Maybe what I miss is the fearless, the careless, the curious, the foolish versions of myself. Those people, those places, and those events reflected the versions of me that I cherished.

Perhaps this is why we can never relive our past, even though we revisit the same place with the same people. That version of us is gone forever; we have long left it behind.
