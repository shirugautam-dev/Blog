# We Have Johatsu Everywhere


![](../Images/articles/johatsu.jpg)
### 
Whenever I think about my childhood, one memory keeps on coming back to me. That is of Bhotu Kaka. He disappeared from our village without any explanation. Just a few days back, I came across a Japanese term, Johatsu, which is given to people who leave without a trace, and then I thought that is what he would be called if he were in Japan.
When my maternal grandfather retired from his job and was looking for a place to settle down as he wanted to avoid the crushing winters of Kathmandu, he chose a small village, Simara, which is 20 kilometres from India border. The village was not known to many then. My grandparents were among the first dwellers.

Every year till middle school, my brother and I packed our bags as soon as the winter holidays of 50 days began and left for Simara. There were only a few houses then, but enough children to play with.
We had a big house, and an even bigger courtyard. Ours was the only house with electricity and a radio. During the daytime, my grandfather tuned it to Binaca or Ceylon channels, and we grooved to Bollywood songs. In the evening, after dinner, everyone from the village came to our place. My grandfather played harmonium and sang bhajans. Someone would grab a manjeera and another a kartaal. My uncle would play TomTom. Children danced, and the rest clapped. Every evening was a celebration.

That’s where I met Bhotu Kaka each winter. He had come with his family to Simara a couple of years after my grandparents. He was a middle-aged man, short but well-built and always in a grey colour daura suruwal, black waistcoat and black Nepali topi. In the afternoons, I saw him smoking beedi and lost in thought. He brought small, round candies that made our lips red. Sometimes he gave us 25 paise each, and we ran to the nearby shop to buy more of those candies.

But one day, without telling anyone, he took some clothes, some cash and left. No one, including his family, knew the reason. There was no note. no promise of coming back. He didn’t seem sad, nor was he in debt. He had enough farmland to feed his family. His family looked for him everywhere they could think of. They asked people in Kathmandu and in Dhading, his ancestral hometown. But he could not be found. His family finally gave up.
Was he exhausted by the expectations of the family and the society that he needed to run away to free himself? I have always wondered.

In Japan, Johatsu, which means evaporation, is someone who leaves home without a trace, at his own will. And this does not happen only in Japan; it happens everywhere. The only difference is that in Japan, he is protected by law. If an adult disappears of his own will, he cannot be forced to be found, and there are agencies that help him settle in a new place with a new identity.
In India, people disappear all the time. According to data.gov.in, annually, lakhs of people are registered as missing. In 2024, around five hundred thousand people were reported missing. Some are found alive, some dead, and some remain disappeared.
 
People in India disappear for many reasons. Trafficking, forced labour, and personal circumstances are among the few. Some leave home due to heavy debt, some adolescents run away due to abuse, academic pressure or elopement.

For some, disappearing can be the only way out, to be free from the burden of the past. This can seem like the only viable, though extreme, solution.

People from villages and small towns settle in megacities like Delhi, Mumbai and Bangalore without ever being found by their families. Unlike in Japan, there is no law or agency helping them to live this altered life. They are not found simply because these cities are so densely populated that they provide cover automatically.

But now with digitisation and the need to register oneself everywhere, how long our Johatsu can remain hidden is not clear.

Should the law protect those adults who have decision-making capacity, act freely and leave voluntarily? They have, after all, the right of personal autonomy and privacy. If one does not want to be found, should he be found? Should not there be a way for that person to confidentially inform the authorities that they are fine but do not wish to be found?

Like in Japan, should we have agencies that help them build a new life in a new place? How would they escape abusive partners or the weight of societal judgment otherwise?

There are so many questions in my mind. But the answers are not that straightforward. I know there are ethical issues. How can a state provide a way for individuals if they are running away from financial or familial responsibilities? How will a society function if people abandon their families and the state helps them do that?
Verifying that there is no criminality involved, the mental capacity of the person and the extent of emotional harm to the families left behind are mammoth tasks.

Still, it does not feel right that a person has all the rights but no right to live anonymously.

Perhaps the question should not be how to help those who want to disappear, but why so many feel that disappearing is the only option.

Perhaps we need a society that is compassionate, offers understanding and expects less for it to produce less Johatsu.

As for Bhotu Kaka, he came back after four years. He went away on his own and came back on his own. He must have told his family why he left. Unfortunately, I still do not know. He is a Johatsu I met even before I knew the term and I keep thinking about him.
